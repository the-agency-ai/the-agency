'/'><img src = '/mf.jpg' title = 'Photo of Martin Fowler'></img></a></p>\n\n<p class = 'name'><a href = '/' rel = 'author'>Martin Fowler</a></p>\n</div>\n\n<div class = 'spacer'></div>\n\n<div class = 'tags'>\n<p class = 'tag-link'><a href = /tags/enterprise%20architecture.html>enterprise architecture</a></p>\n\n<p class = 'tag-link'><a href = /tags/collaboration.html>collaboration</a></p>\n\n<p class = 'tag-link'><a href = /tags/application%20architecture.html>application architecture</a></p>\n</div>\n</div>\n\n<div class = 'clear'></div>\n\n<div class = 'paperBody'>\n<p>An Architecture Decision Record (ADR) is a short document that captures and\n explains a single decision relevant to a product or ecosystem. Documents\n should be short, just a couple of pages, and contain the decision, the context\n for making it, and significant ramifications. They should not be modified if\n the decision is changed, but linked to a superseding decision.</p>\n\n<p>As with most written documents, writing ADRs serves two purposes. Firstly they\n act as a record of decisions, allowing people months or years later to\n understand why the system is constructed in the way that it is. But perhaps\n even more valuable, the act of writing them helps to clarify thinking,\n particularly with groups of people. Writing a document of consequence\n often surfaces different points of view - forcing those differences to be\n discussed, and hopefully resolved.</p>\n\n<p>A general rule is to follow an “inverted pyramid” style of\n writing, commonly associated with news stories. The key is to put the most\n important material at the start, and push details to later in the record.</p>\n\n<p>The common advice is to keep decision records in the source repository of\n the code base to which they apply. A common choice for their location is\n <code>doc/adr</code>. This way they are easily available to those\n working on the code base. For similar reasons they should be written in a\n lightweight markup language, such as markdown, so they can be easily read and\n diffed just like any code. We can use a build task to publish them to a product\n team's website.</p>\n\n<p>Storing them in a product repository won't work for ADRs that cover a broader\n ecosystem than a single code base. Some folks also feel that keeping ADRs in\n git makes it too hard for non-developers to work with them.</p>\n\n<p>Each record should be its own file, and should be numbered in a monotonic\n sequence as part of their file name, with a name that captures the decision,\n so that they are easy to read in a directory listing. (for example:\n “<code>0001-HTMX-for-active-web-pages</code>“).</p>\n\n<p>Each ADR has a status. “proposed” while it is under discussion, “accepted”\n once the team accepts it and it is active, “superseded” once it is\n significantly modified or replaced - with a link to the superseding ADR. Once\n an ADR is accepted, it should never be reopened or changed - instead it should be\n superseded. That way we have a clear log of decisions and how long they\n governed the work.</p>\n\n<p>ADRs contain not just the decision, but also a brief rationale for the\n decision. This should summarize the problem that led to this decision being\n needed and the trade-offs that were taken into account. A good way to think of\n them follows the notion of “forces” when writing a pattern. As part of this\n it's valuable to explicitly list all the serious alternatives that were\n considered, together with their pros and cons.</p>\n\n<p>Any decision has consequences. Sometimes these are clearly implied from the\n rationale, but sometimes it's worth clearly stating them in a explicit\n section. Decisions are usually made under some degree of uncertainty, so it's\n handy to record the confidence level of the decision. This is a good place\n to mention any changes in the product context that should trigger the team to\n reevaluate the decision.</p>\n\n<p>ADRs play a central role in the <a href = 'https://martinfowler.com/articles/scaling-architecture-conversationally.html'>Advice Process</a>,\n where they are not only used to document decisions, but the act of writing\n them is used to elicit expertise and alignment. In this case they should also\n include advice gathered in forming the ADR, although in order to keep things\n brief, it may be better to summarize the advice in the ADR and keep a full\n record of advice separately.</p>\n\n<p>The most important thing to bear in mind here is brevity. Keep the ADR\n short and to the point - typically a single page. If there's supporting\n material, link to it.</p>\n\n<p>While ADRs are a form for recording decisions in software architecture, the\n broader concept of writing short decision records is worth considering in\n other contexts. This kind of decision log creates a valuable historic record\n that can do much to explain why things are the way they turned out.</p>\n\n<div class = 'furtherReading'>\n<h2>Further Reading</h2>\n\n<p>Michael Nygard coined the term “Architecture Decision Record” with an\n <a href = 'https://cognitect.com/blog/2011/11/15/documenting-architecture-decisions'>ADR-formatted article</a> in 2011. While he did not\n originate the idea of a decision log he did\n make case for a lightweight document, with a focus on the decision\n itself. In this he was particularly inspired by\n Phillipe Kruchten talking about decision registers / decision logs, and by\n the writing style of <a href = '/articles/writingPatterns.html'>software patterns</a>. His\n article is better than pretty much everything else written on the topic, my only\n desire to write this one was to point to some developments since.</p>\n\n<p>On this site, there are brief examples of ADR formats in articles by <a href = 'https://martinfowler.com/articles/scaling-architecture-conversationally.html#adr'>Harmel-Law</a> and <a href = 'https://martinfowler.com/articles/building-infrastructure-platform.html#ArchitecturalDecisionRecords'>Rowse and Shepherd</a>. </p>\n\n<p><a href = 'https://github.com/npryce/adr-tools'>adr-tools</a> is a simple command line tool to\n manage ADRs. It includes a set of ADRs for itself that are a good example of\n the form.</p>\n</div>\n\n<div class = 'acknowledgements'>\n<h2>Acknowledgements</h2>\n\n<p>Andrew Harmel-Law, Brandon Cook, David Lucas, Francisco Dias, Giuseppe Matheus Pereira, John King, Kief Morris, Michael Joyce, Neil Price, Shane\n Gibson, Steven Peh, and Vijay\n Raghavan Aravamudhan discussed drafts of this post on\n our internal chat.
