Most consumer health companies aren't building moats. They're building products.

The difference matters. A product can be copied, especially in the age of AI. A moat can't. And in consumer health, where distribution is commoditized and trust erodes fast, the question of what's actually defensible is more urgent than most founders want to admit.

I've been thinking through this. Here's what I actually believe creates durable competitive advantage — beyond brand:

**1. Proprietary data**
Data that either no one else can collect or it's extremely difficult to. If your product generates a longitudinal dataset that improves with scale — and competitors can't replicate it — you have something. Most "data moats" in health are actually just databases.

**2. Hardware or deep technical expertise**
Software is replicable. A proprietary sensor, a manufacturing process, a clinical-grade measurement tool — those take years to build and are expensive to copy. The companies with hardware at their core are playing a different game.

**3. Reimbursement unlock**
If you can show — with rigorous data — that your product improves health outcomes, you become fundable by insurers, self-insured employers, and eventually government programs. That's a structural revenue shift most DTC companies can't access. Outcomes data isn't a feature. It's a business model.

**4. Actual behavior change**
The hardest one. The market is full of products that inform. Almost none of them change what people do. The companies that crack durable behavior change don't just track habits — they engineer the conditions that make behavior inevitable. That's an IP-worthy capability.

Most companies have zero of these. A few have one. Rare is the team building toward two or more.
